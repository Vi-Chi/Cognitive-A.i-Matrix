That's a solid engineering decision on multiple levels.

Center of mass mounting means the box experiences the least angular acceleration when the boat rolls or pitches. Everything else on the boat swings around that point — the mast base is as close to the vessel's motion center as you can get without being in the keel.

Dry and protected — the cabin ceiling at the mast base is about as sheltered as it gets. No spray ingress, no bilge humidity, away from hatches.

The 90° steel tube bracket off the mast base is clever because:

The mast already transfers enormous compression loads into the boat's structure — it's the most solidly connected point in the hull
Welding a bracket to that tube means the box is effectively hanging from the backbone of the vessel
Vibration will be transferred but it's low-frequency structural vibration, not high-shock impacts
A few things worth thinking through for the bracket design:

Isolation — you'll want rubber or polyurethane bushings between the steel bracket and the box itself. Electronics and hard-mounted steel in a marine environment is a bad combination for vibration fatigue on solder joints and connectors over time.

Cable routing — hanging from the ceiling means power and data runs go up, which is actually good for water ingress protection on connectors.

Access — can you open the box for servicing without unbolting the bracket? Hinged front panel?

Good concrete question — and a useful gear shift.

Let me give you a honest map of the processing unit landscape.

The main categories:

CPU — general purpose, low parallelism, high per-core performance. Good at sequential logic, control flow, running an OS. Intel/AMD x86, ARM Cortex. Measured in GHz and IPC, not FLOPS primarily.

GPU — massively parallel, thousands of simpler cores. Originally graphics, now dominates matrix math which is what neural networks need. NVIDIA CUDA ecosystem dominates. Measured in TFLOPS for FP32/FP16/INT8.

NPU — Neural Processing Unit, purpose-built for inference. Fixed function dataflow architecture, extremely efficient per-watt for specific tensor operations. Hailo, Google Coral, Apple Neural Engine. Measured in TOPS (Tera Operations Per Second, typically INT8).

The topology difference is fundamental:

CPUs are von Neumann — fetch, decode, execute, writeback. Sequential by design, caches everywhere to hide memory latency.

GPUs are SIMD — Single Instruction Multiple Data. Same instruction executed across thousands of cores simultaneously. Memory bandwidth is the bottleneck, not compute.

NPUs are dataflow — data moves through fixed computational graphs. No fetch/decode overhead. Extremely efficient but inflexible — optimized for inference, not training.

For your specific constraints — low power, offline, maritime:

The Pi5 + Hailo-10H is actually a well-chosen combination. The Pi5 handles control flow and orchestration, Hailo handles inference. They're complementary architectures.
